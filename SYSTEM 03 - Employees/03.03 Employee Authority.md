## Authority Philosophy

Authority is the power to make decisions within defined scope. Clear authority empowers employees to act; unclear authority creates confusion and delays.

**Core Principle**: Authority should be as close to work as possible. Decisions should be made by those doing the work or directly supervising it, not by remote leadership.

## Authority Framework

### Principle 1: Clarity

Every employee knows:
- What decisions they can make independently
- What decisions require input
- What decisions require approval
- Who to consult
- How to escalate

### Principle 2: Competence-Based

Authority is based on:
- Experience and judgment
- Proven decision quality
- Understanding of context
- Stakeholder impact knowledge

### Principle 3: Proportional

Authority matches:
- Level in organization
- Responsibility scope
- Impact of decisions
- Trust demonstrated

### Principle 4: Escalation

Clear escalation path for:
- High-impact decisions
- Precedent-setting decisions
- Complex decisions
- Conflicting stakeholders

## Authority Levels

### Level 1: Direct Authority

**Definition**: Can make decision without consulting anyone

**Who**: Individual contributors in their area of responsibility

**Examples**:
- How to approach assigned task
- Which tool or method to use
- When to work (within schedule)
- How to organize their work
- Technical decisions within scope

**Constraints**:
- Must follow established standards
- Must meet quality criteria
- Must keep team informed
- Must escalate risks

**Scope**: Individual task level

### Level 2: Consultation Authority

**Definition**: Can make decision after consulting appropriate parties

**Who**: Team leads, experienced contributors

**Examples**:
- How to allocate team time
- Which approach for team project
- Team performance issues
- Resource requests for team
- Process improvements in team

**Consultation Requirements**:
- Consult team members
- Consult manager
- Consider cross-functional impact
- Escalate if conflicts arise

**Scope**: Team level

### Level 3: Approval Authority

**Definition**: Can make decision but needs formal approval from higher level

**Who**: Managers, department heads

**Examples**:
- Hiring decisions
- Significant budget changes
- Major process changes
- New initiatives
- Performance management decisions

**Approval Requirements**:
- Document rationale
- Provide analysis
- Consider impacts
- Get explicit approval
- Escalate disagreements

**Scope**: Department/functional level

### Level 4: Executive Authority

**Definition**: Executive leadership decision

**Who**: Executive team, C-suite

**Examples**:
- Strategic direction
- Major investments
- Organizational structure
- Significant policy changes
- Executive compensation
- Major partnerships

**Decision Process**:
- Comprehensive analysis
- Stakeholder input
- Option evaluation
- Board/stakeholder approval if needed
- Clear communication

**Scope**: Organizational level

## Authority by Role

### Individual Contributor

**Decision Authority**:
- How to do assigned work
- Which tools and methods to use
- Schedule within flexibility
- Process improvements in their work
- Asking for help or resources

**Escalation**: To team lead or manager

**Constraints**:
- Follow standards
- Meet expectations
- Communicate status
- Don't commit on behalf of team
- Don't make decisions affecting others

### Team Lead

**Decision Authority**:
- How to organize team work
- Work allocation across team
- Performance feedback and coaching
- Discipline for performance issues
- Minor process changes
- Team meeting agendas
- Vendor selection (within approved vendors)

**Escalation**: To manager or leadership

**Constraints**:
- Must align with organizational standards
- Significant changes require approval
- Must consider equity across team
- Must document decisions
- Hiring decisions go to manager

### Manager

**Decision Authority**:
- Hiring and firing
- Budget allocation
- Project approval
- Resource allocation
- Compensation recommendations
- Significant process changes
- Cross-team coordination
- Performance management

**Escalation**: To senior leadership

**Constraints**:
- Follow organizational policies
- Significant changes need approval
- Must document decisions
- Budget constraints
- Equity requirements
- Policy compliance

### Executive Leadership

**Decision Authority**:
- Strategic direction
- Major investments
- Organizational structure
- Executive hiring/compensation
- Major policy changes
- Significant partnerships
- Crisis decisions
- Organizational priorities

**Escalation**: To board or stakeholders if needed

**Constraints**:
- Board oversight
- Regulatory compliance
- Stakeholder considerations
- Governance requirements
- Legal constraints

## Decision Categories

### Routine Decisions

**Authority**: Individual can decide

**Examples**:
- How to approach task
- When to schedule work
- Which tool to use
- How to organize work
- Day-to-day operations

**Process**: Decide and execute

### Standard Decisions

**Authority**: Team lead or manager can decide

**Examples**:
- Work allocation
- Schedule adjustments
- Process improvements
- Resource requests
- Performance feedback

**Process**: Decide after appropriate consultation

### Important Decisions

**Authority**: Manager or above

**Examples**:
- Hiring and firing
- Significant budget changes
- New initiatives
- Major process changes
- Organizational changes

**Process**: Analysis, consultation, approval

### Strategic Decisions

**Authority**: Executive leadership

**Examples**:
- Strategic direction
- Major investments
- Organizational structure
- Strategic partnerships
- Major policy changes

**Process**: Comprehensive analysis, stakeholder consultation, formal approval

## Escalation Process

### When to Escalate

Escalate when:
- Decision exceeds your authority
- Decision impacts other areas
- Significant risk or uncertainty
- Precedent-setting decision
- Conflicting stakeholder needs
- High impact or visibility

### Escalation Path

**Individual Contributor**: → Team Lead → Manager → Leadership

**Team Lead**: → Manager → Director → Leadership

**Manager**: → Director → Executive → Board

### Escalation Conversation

**Include**:
- Context and background
- Analysis of options
- Recommendation
- Risk assessment
- Deadline for decision
- Stakeholder input

## Authority and Accountability

**Key Principle**: Authority comes with accountability

**This Means**:
- Decision outcomes are your responsibility
- You must monitor and adjust
- You must report results
- You must learn from outcomes
- You must be transparent

**Accountability Measures**:
- Were results achieved?
- Was process appropriate?
- Were stakeholders considered?
- Was decision quality good?
- Were learnings captured?

## Developing Authority

### Authority Grows Through

1. **Demonstrated Judgment**: Making good decisions consistently
2. **Trust Building**: Earning trust through reliability
3. **Experience**: Understanding context and implications
4. **Growth**: Taking on expanded responsibilities
5. **Development**: Building decision-making capability

### Supporting Authority Development

**Organization provides**:
- Clear expectations
- Feedback on decisions
- Opportunity to decide
- Support and coaching
- Mentorship

**Employee provides**:
- Thoughtful decision-making
- Learning from outcomes
- Asking for guidance
- Building relationships
- Continuous improvement

## Authority Misuse

### When Authority Is Misused

**Misuse includes**:
- Decisions exceeding scope
- Ignoring established standards
- Disregarding stakeholder impact
- Poor decision process
- Accountability avoidance

**Consequences**:
- Loss of authority
- Performance management
- Escalation to leadership
- Potential termination (severe cases)

### Preventing Misuse

**Organization**:
- Clear authority frameworks
- Training and guidance
- Regular feedback
- Accountability systems

**Manager**:
- Oversight and feedback
- Course correction
- Support and coaching
- Clear expectations

**Employee**:
- Understanding authority
- Seeking guidance
- Thoughtful decision-making
- Accepting accountability
